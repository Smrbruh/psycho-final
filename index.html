01

Introduction to Psychology

The scientific study of mind, mental processes, and behavior

Lecture Overview

Psychology is one of the most practically useful sciences because it bridges our inner world with outer behavior. This lecture establishes the foundation of the entire course: what psychology is, where it came from, what it studies, and how its insights apply to everyday student life.

The major insight is that psychology began as philosophy — a study of the soul — and transformed over 200 years into a rigorous scientific discipline studying observable behavior and measurable mental processes. Every other lecture in this course builds upon these foundations.

Practically, understanding psychology makes you more effective at communication, decision-making, learning, leadership, and managing your own health — skills directly applicable at a university in Kazakhstan today.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Psychology
The scientific study of the mind, mental processes, and behavior. From Greek: psyche (soul/mind) + logos (study). Emerged formally as a science when Wilhelm Wundt opened his laboratory in Leipzig in 1879.
Behavior
The external expression of internal mental processes — all the actions we produce to express our thoughts, needs, and wants. Behavior is observable and measurable.
Mental Processes
Internal activities of the mind including thoughts, beliefs, feelings, perceptions, memory, and reasoning. These cannot be directly observed but can be inferred from behavior.
Consciousness
Awareness of one's own thoughts, feelings, and environment. Psychologists distinguish between conscious (accessible) and unconscious (hidden) mental processes.
Psychotherapy
A branch of psychology where a trained therapist works with a client through conversation to understand and resolve psychological problems. Based on the insight that problems we create, we can also resolve.
Scientific Method in Psychology
The systematic process of forming hypotheses, designing experiments, collecting data, and drawing conclusions about psychological phenomena. Distinguishes modern psychology from philosophical speculation.

Branches of Psychology

General Psychology

Studies feelings, mental processes, and universal aspects of human behavior. The core branch from which all others grow.

Educational Psychology

How people learn, acquire knowledge, and retain information. Designs methods to improve learning outcomes.

Developmental Psychology

Studies growth across the lifespan from birth to old age — the psychological, cognitive, and social changes at each stage.

Social Psychology

Studies communication and behavior in social contexts — groups, families, friendships, and culture. Includes sub-fields of family, gender, group, and health psychology.

Health Psychology

Studies the relationship between psychological factors and physical health. Grew from sports psychology. Explores how mind and body interact.

Clinical & Abnormal Psychology

Studies, diagnoses, and treats mental disorders. Works alongside psychiatry to support psychological well-being.

Cognitive Psychology

Studies internal mental processes: memory, problem-solving, perception, language, and decision-making.

Cyberpsychology

Studies how the internet, digital technology, and social media affect human behavior, identity, relationships, and mental health — increasingly relevant for contemporary university students.

Key Thinkers & Scholars

Wilhelm Wundt
1832–1920
Founded the first psychological laboratory (Leipzig, 1879).

Structuralism — believed consciousness could be studied by breaking it into its basic elements through introspection. Made psychology a formal science.

William James
1842–1910
Father of American Psychology. Author of Principles of Psychology (1890).

Functionalism — focused on the purpose and function of mental processes rather than their structure. Created influential learning methods for everyday use.

Sigmund Freud
1856–1939
Founded psychoanalysis and psychotherapy.

Divided personality into Id (instinct), Ego (reason), and Superego (moral ideal). Argued that unconscious drives and early childhood experiences shape adult behavior.

B.F. Skinner
1904–1990
Pioneer of Behaviorism and Operant Conditioning.

Argued that behavior is shaped by its consequences — reinforcement increases behavior, punishment decreases it. Developed the famous Skinner Box.

Albert Bandura
1925–2021
Social Learning Theory & Self-Efficacy.

Humans learn by observing others. Introduced the concept of self-efficacy — our belief in our ability to succeed — which profoundly shapes motivation and behavior.

Jean Piaget
1896–1980
Stages of Cognitive Development.

Children move through four cognitive stages: sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational. Biology and environment interact in development.

Lev Vygotsky
1896–1934
Zone of Proximal Development; Social Constructivism.

Social interaction is fundamental to cognitive development. Children learn within the ZPD — the gap between what they can do alone and with guidance.

Carl Rogers
1902–1987
Humanistic Psychology; Person-Centered Therapy.

Emphasized unconditional positive regard, empathy, and authenticity in therapy. Introduced the concepts of ideal self, self-image, and self-worth.

Ivan Pavlov
1849–1936
Classical Conditioning.

Demonstrated that neutral stimuli could be conditioned to produce learned responses. Foundational for understanding how habits, attitudes, and fears are formed.

Erik Erikson
1902–1994
Eight Stages of Psychosocial Development.

Each life stage presents a specific crisis to resolve — from trust vs. mistrust in infancy to ego integrity vs. despair in old age.

Real-Life Examples

University Life

When you study for exams, educational psychology explains why spaced repetition beats cramming — your brain consolidates memory during rest and sleep.

Digital Life

Social media algorithms exploit behaviorist principles — likes and notifications act as variable-ratio reinforcement schedules (the most addictive type), keeping you scrolling.

Kazakhstan Context

The sociocultural perspective explains why Kazakhstani students navigate between collectivist family values and individualistic university achievement demands simultaneously.

Cyberpsychology

Online identity is studied through the psychodynamic lens — people use avatars and social media profiles to project their Ideal Self while managing their actual self-image.

🎓 Likely Exam Questions

  1. What is psychology? Define it and explain its origin as a scientific discipline.

    Model Answer

    Psychology is the scientific study of the mind, mental processes, and behavior. The word derives from the Greek psyche (mind/soul) and logos (study). Although the word was first used by philosopher Rudolf Goeckel in 1590, psychology became a formal science when Wilhelm Wundt opened the first psychological laboratory in Leipzig, Germany in 1879, marking the transition from philosophical speculation to empirical science.

  2. Name and describe three major branches of psychology.

    Model Answer

    General Psychology studies universal feelings, mental processes, and behavior applicable to all people. Developmental Psychology examines how people grow and change across the lifespan, identifying key stages and challenges. Social Psychology focuses on communication in social contexts — in groups, families, and broader cultures — including sub-fields like family, gender, and health psychology.

  3. Compare behaviorism and the humanistic perspective in psychology.

    Model Answer

    Behaviorism (Skinner, Watson) focuses exclusively on observable behavior, arguing that all behavior is a product of environmental conditioning through reinforcement and punishment. It largely ignores internal mental states. Humanism (Maslow, Rogers) takes the opposite view — it emphasizes internal experience, personal growth, free will, and the drive toward self-actualization. Where behaviorism is deterministic, humanism is optimistic about human agency.

  4. Why is psychology relevant to everyday life? Give three examples.

    Model Answer

    Psychology is relevant because it provides tools for self-understanding and effective living. First, understanding motivation helps explain why we procrastinate and how to overcome it. Second, knowing communication psychology makes us more effective in relationships and group settings. Third, health psychology teaches that many illnesses originate from stress and negative mental states, enabling preventive action.

02

Personality: Theories, Traits, and Self-Regulation

What makes you uniquely you — and how to manage your inner world

Lecture Overview

Personality is the characteristic pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that makes each person unique. It is not fixed at birth but develops dynamically, generally stabilizing by around age 25. This lecture explores the determinants, theories, and measurement of personality, along with the crucial skill of self-regulation — how we monitor and manage our inner states to navigate life effectively.

Understanding your own personality helps you predict your behavior, understand your relationships, and make better decisions. Self-regulation is what allows you to stay on track even when emotions and impulses pull you elsewhere.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Personality
The characteristic pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that makes each person unique. Dynamic — develops over time, generally formed by age 25.
Temperament
The biological, largely inherited basis of personality — our natural emotional reactivity and intensity. Described by Eysenck using sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic types.
Self-Regulation
The ability to monitor and manage emotions, thoughts, and behavior in ways that serve well-being, new learning, and healthy relationships. Has physical, emotional, mental, and social dimensions.
Ideal Self
(Rogers) The person we aspire to become. When the gap between ideal self and actual self-image is large, psychological tension results.
Self-Image
(Rogers) How we currently perceive ourselves — our self-concept in the present moment.
Self-Worth
(Rogers) The core values we hold about ourselves — what we believe is fundamentally good and important about who we are.
Psychometric Tests
Standardized assessments used to measure psychological traits, including personality dimensions. Introduced formally by Eysenck.

Five Determinants of Personality

  1. Biological Factors — body structure, genes, brain architecture, and neurochemistry shape fundamental tendencies and temperament.
  2. Cultural Factors — cultural rules, values, and norms absorbed from the social environment. We often adopt behaviors simply because those around us in our culture do the same.
  3. Family Factors — the most influential first social group. Communication patterns, knowledge, beliefs, and behaviors observed in family become internalized and carried into adult life.
  4. Social Factors — broader social environment including peers, institutions, media, and community. Changes in social environment produce personality change.
  5. Situational Factors — specific life situations provide self-knowledge. Crisis, success, loss, and challenge all shape who we become.

The Big Five (Five-Factor Model — OCEAN)

Trait High Score Means Low Score Means Related to
Openness Curious, imaginative, open to new experiences Conventional, prefers routine Creativity, learning
Conscientiousness Disciplined, organized, morally committed Impulsive, disorganized Academic & professional success
Extraversion Sociable, outgoing, energized by others Introverted, prefers solitude Leadership, communication
Agreeableness Cooperative, warm, empathetic Competitive, antagonistic Relationships, teamwork
Neuroticism Emotionally reactive, prone to stress Emotionally stable, calm Mental health, coping

Mnemonic: OCEAN — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.

Major Theories of Personality

Freud — Psychodynamic

Id: Pleasure principle, instinct, no morality.
Ego: Reality principle, rational navigation of society.
Superego: Moral ideal — striving for the greatest good for all.

Eysenck — Dimensions

Two axes: Stability↔Neuroticism and Extraversion↔Introversion. Combined, these produce four temperaments: Sanguine (stable/extrovert), Choleric (neurotic/extrovert), Phlegmatic (stable/introvert), Melancholic (neurotic/introvert).

Cattell — 16PF

Argued personality cannot be reduced to a few types — identified 16 personality factors (16PF). Behavior expresses different aspects of personality depending on social context and situation.

Rogers — Humanistic

Only we can create our personality. Three self components: Ideal Self (who we want to be), Self-Image (who we are now), Self-Worth (what we value at our core). Congruence between these is mental health.

Erikson — Psychosocial Stages

Personality develops through 8 life stages. Each stage presents a key conflict: e.g., Trust vs. Mistrust (infancy), Identity vs. Role Confusion (adolescence), Intimacy vs. Isolation (young adult).

Skinner — Behavioral

Childhood experiences form the foundation of adult personality. A rich environment with good communication, knowledge, and healthy family relationships builds effective adult behavior patterns.

Self-Regulation: Four Aspects

Physical Aspect

Biology, body, genes, brain structure, and temperament — the biological substrate of our emotional reactions.

Emotional Aspect

Personality, impulses, and habits. Habits are automatic behavior patterns — understanding them allows us to change or replace them.

Mental Aspect

Self-control, mental management, and effective functioning in social environments. The cognitive side of managing ourselves.

Social Aspect

Rules, values, empathy — everything that enables us to communicate and build a good life within a social context.

Self-Regulation Methods

  • Planning: Life plan, 5-year plan, monthly plan, daily plan. From time management to life management.
  • Monitoring: Continuously assess your life stage, challenges, and available resources.
  • Reflection: Think about what you have done, what you are doing, and what you need next.
  • Using Feedback: Regularly ask — what do I need now? What am I doing? What do I want to do?
  • Improving Communication: Recognize problems in your communication and grow through them.

Evening Self-Reflection Practice

  • What happened today?
  • How did I react to situations?
  • How did others react?
  • What were the reasons for these situations?
  • What else could I have done?
  • What good things did I do today?

🎓 Likely Exam Questions

  1. Describe the Big Five model of personality. What does each trait measure?

    Model Answer

    The Big Five (OCEAN) is the most empirically validated personality model. Openness measures curiosity and openness to new experiences. Conscientiousness measures discipline and reliability. Extraversion measures sociability and energy from others. Agreeableness measures warmth and cooperation. Neuroticism measures emotional instability and stress reactivity. Each dimension exists on a spectrum, not as a binary.

  2. What is self-regulation and why is it important for university students?

    Model Answer

    Self-regulation is the ability to monitor and manage one's emotions, thoughts, and behaviors to support well-being and goal attainment. It has four dimensions: physical (biology and temperament), emotional (habits and impulses), mental (self-control and cognitive management), and social (values, empathy, and communication norms). For university students, self-regulation is crucial for managing academic pressure, social relationships, deadlines, and the competing demands of personal goals and social expectations.

03

Motivation & Goal-Directed Behavior

Why we act — and how to keep moving toward what matters

Lecture Overview

Motivation is the engine of human behavior. Without it, we do nothing. This lecture explores what motivation is, the major theories explaining it, the difference between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, and — critically — how to build and sustain self-motivation. The lecture connects motivation directly to goals, habits, and the cycle of growth.

The word motivation comes from the Latin movere — to move. A motivated person is always active. Understanding motivation is one of the most practically powerful areas of psychology.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Motivation
A process (not a state) involving behavior, goals, and direction. Activated by needs, it includes biological, emotional, social, and cognitive components. From Latin movere = to move.
Motive
A single unit of motivation — a specific need, want, or interest that drives action. All behavior has a motive.
Intrinsic Motivation
Motivation arising from internal needs, values, and personal meaning. Stable, powerful, produces genuine emotional satisfaction. Examples: curiosity, love of learning, personal growth.
Extrinsic Motivation
Motivation from external sources — money, recognition, social approval, grades. Can drive behavior but rarely produces deep satisfaction.
Self-Efficacy
(Bandura) Belief in one's ability to achieve a specific goal. High self-efficacy increases persistence and performance. Central to self-motivation.
Self-Actualization
(Maslow) The highest human need — to express one's full potential and contribute to others. The apex of the needs hierarchy.

Types of Motivation

Type Source Stability Examples
Intrinsic Internal needs & values High — sustainable Love, curiosity, meaning, learning
Extrinsic External rewards Variable — fades without reward Money, grades, recognition, status
Automatic Habit & routine Stable once formed Morning exercise, daily study routine
Non-Regulated External pressure, no engagement Very low Doing a task only to avoid punishment

Theories of Motivation

Content Theories (What Motivates Us)

Maslow's Hierarchy (1943)

  1. Physiological (food, water, sleep)
  2. Safety (security, stability)
  3. Belonging (love, group membership)
  4. Esteem (recognition, status)
  5. Self-Actualization (full potential)

Each level is a goal. Meeting lower needs creates motivation to pursue higher ones.

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Motivators: Growth, recognition, meaningful work, achievement — produce satisfaction.
Hygiene Factors: Salary, relationships, working conditions — prevent dissatisfaction but don't create motivation alone.

Alderfer's ERG Model

Existence (physiological + safety)
Relatedness (social connection)
Growth (development + actualization)

These form a continuous cycle — growth leads to new needs, which starts the process again.

Process Theories (How Motivation Works)

Expectancy Theory (Vroom)

Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence. We are motivated when we believe our effort will lead to performance, performance will lead to reward, and the reward is valuable to us.

Equity Theory (Adams)

Motivation is affected by perceived fairness. When we feel we are receiving outcomes proportional to our inputs (compared to others), we are motivated. Inequity demotivates.

Goal-Setting Theory (Locke & Latham)

Specific, challenging goals produce higher motivation and performance than vague or easy goals. Goals must be clear, measurable, and meaningful. High goals → high motivation.

The Motivation Cycle

  1. Need / New Idea
  2. Goal Formation
  3. Positive Emotion & Focus
  4. Active Behavior
  5. New Habits
  6. Success / Result
  7. New Need / Idea (cycle repeats)

If you lose a step, return to the beginning. Start with a new idea or need and restart the cycle.

Building Self-Motivation

Only about 15% of motivation comes from external sources. Internal needs — though fewer — are more powerful and produce more genuine positive emotion.

Practical Techniques

  • Use time management and goal-setting systems
  • Read and watch motivating content
  • Spend time with people who have achieved things
  • Use NLP: choose words deliberately when setting goals
  • Visualize your ideal life — create a mental vision board
  • Change your social environment toward growth-oriented people
  • Stay active — activity itself generates new motivation
  • Compete with yourself — be better than yesterday

Key Scholars for Lecture 3

Abraham Maslow
1908–1970
Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

Five-level pyramid from physiological survival to self-actualization. Humanistic approach to motivation — each satisfied need motivates pursuit of the next level.

Albert Bandura
Self-Efficacy Theory

Belief in your own capacity is the single strongest predictor of motivation. Self-efficacy is built through mastery experiences, role models, social encouragement, and physiological state management.

Deci & Ryan
Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

Three innate needs power intrinsic motivation: Competence (mastery), Relatedness (belonging), and Autonomy (self-direction). When all three are met, intrinsic motivation flourishes.

🎓 Likely Exam Questions

  1. Explain Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs and its relevance to student motivation.

    Model Answer

    Maslow proposed that human needs form a pyramid with five levels. The bottom level — physiological needs (food, sleep, water) — must be met before higher levels can motivate us. Above that: safety, then belonging and love, then esteem (recognition, status), and finally self-actualization (full potential). For students: if basic needs aren't met (sleep deprivation, poor nutrition), academic motivation (esteem, actualization) suffers. Meeting belonging needs through peer connection supports the motivation to achieve.

  2. Compare intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Which is more effective and why?

    Model Answer

    Intrinsic motivation arises from internal values, curiosity, love, and personal meaning. Extrinsic motivation comes from external rewards — money, grades, or social recognition. Research shows intrinsic motivation is generally more powerful because it is self-sustaining, produces deeper emotional satisfaction, and survives when external rewards are removed. Extrinsic motivation can be useful for getting started but may undermine long-term intrinsic interest (overjustification effect). The most motivated people connect their external activities to internal values.

04

Emotions and Emotional Intelligence (EQ)

Understanding, managing, and using emotions for a better life

Lecture Overview

Emotions are the first layer of our relationship with the world. They arise before thought, before language — as immediate signals telling us what is happening. This lecture explores what emotions are, how they differ from feelings, the theories explaining their origin and function, and — crucially — how to develop Emotional Intelligence (EQ): the ability to perceive, use, understand, and manage emotions.

In an age of social media and digital communication, EQ has become more important than ever. Online environments intensify emotions while reducing the nonverbal cues that help us understand them. Developing EQ is one of the highest-value skills a university student can build.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Emotion
A short cognitive and psychological response to an external stimulus. Lasts seconds to minutes. The first signal giving us information about our environment and the people within it. Over 300 emotions identified by science.
Feeling
A longer-lasting state shaped by personal beliefs and memory. Feelings can persist for hours, days, or longer. Emotions from our environment give rise to feelings, which then color future emotional responses.
Emotional Intelligence (EQ)
The ability to perceive, use, understand, and manage emotions — in ourselves and others. Coined by Salovey & Mayer; popularized by Goleman. Predicts social and professional success often better than IQ.
Empathy
The ability to understand and share the feelings of another person. Central to EQ and effective relationships. Distinguished from sympathy (feeling sorry for someone) — empathy means feeling with them.
Plutchik's Wheel
Robert Plutchik's model of 8 basic emotion pairs: Anger/Fear, Happiness/Sadness, Surprise/Anticipation, Trust/Disgust. All emotions combine and vary in intensity like colors on a wheel.

Emotions vs. Feelings: Key Comparison

Dimension Emotion Feeling
Duration Short (seconds to minutes) Long (hours, days, longer)
Origin External stimulus Internal — shaped by memory & belief
Awareness Often automatic/pre-conscious Usually conscious and reflective
Examples Startle at loud noise, flush of joy Love, grief, resentment, contentment
Role in psychology First signal / information Ongoing interpretation of experience

Plutchik's 8 Basic Emotions

Anger ↔ Fear

Paired opposites. Anger = impulse to move toward a threat; Fear = impulse to move away. Both are survival emotions.

Happiness ↔ Sadness

Paired opposites. Happiness signals success/goal attainment; Sadness signals loss or separation.

Surprise ↔ Anticipation

Paired opposites. Surprise = unexpected stimulus; Anticipation = expectation of a future stimulus.

Trust ↔ Disgust

Paired opposites. Trust = acceptance and connection; Disgust = rejection of a harmful stimulus.

Theories of Emotion

Darwin's Evolutionary Theory

Emotions evolved as adaptive responses. Fear kept ancestors safe; anger mobilized defense; joy reinforced beneficial behaviors. Negative emotions evolved first because survival required it.

James-Lange Theory

We experience emotions as physical reactions first, which the mind then interprets. "We don't tremble because we are afraid — we are afraid because we tremble." Body leads; mind follows.

Cannon-Bard Theory

Body and mind experience emotion simultaneously. The thalamus sends signals to both the cortex (feeling) and the body (reaction) at the same time — not sequentially.

Goleman's 5 Components of Emotional Intelligence

1. Self-Awareness

Knowing and understanding your own emotions as they occur. The foundation of all EQ — you can't manage what you can't recognize.

2. Self-Regulation

Managing and controlling your emotional responses. Choosing how to respond rather than automatically reacting. Includes delaying gratification and controlling impulses.

3. Motivation

Using emotions to drive goal-directed behavior. High-EQ people are intrinsically motivated and use emotional energy as fuel for achievement.

4. Empathy

Understanding and sharing the emotions of others. Empathy is not pity — it requires genuinely perceiving another's emotional state and responding appropriately.

5. Social Skills

Applying emotional understanding to build and maintain effective relationships. Includes communication, conflict resolution, leadership, and collaboration.

IQ vs EQ

Dimension IQ EQ
What it measures Cognitive / analytical ability Emotional understanding & management
Fixity Relatively stable across life Can be developed at any age
Predicts Academic performance Professional success, leadership, relationships
Social value Useful in technical domains Essential in all human interaction

Robert Plutchik

Daniel Goleman

Health Psychology and Stress Management

How the mind creates, maintains, and heals the body

Lecture Overview

Health psychology studies how biological, psychological, behavioral, and social factors together influence health and illness. The central insight of this field is profound: all illness begins in the mind. Negative emotions that cannot be expressed become trapped in the body and — over time — manifest as physical symptoms. This is the field of psychosomatics.

For students in Kazakhstan's high-paced academic environments, understanding stress physiology and management is not abstract — it is daily life. This lecture provides both the scientific framework and practical tools for maintaining mental and physical health.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Health Psychology
A specialized branch studying how biology, psychology, behavior, and social factors interact to influence health and illness. Developed formally around 1917.
Psychosomatics
Physical symptoms that originate from psychological causes — stress, negative emotions, or difficult experiences. "Psyche" = mind; "soma" = body. The body communicates unresolved psychological pain through physical symptoms.
Stress
A psychological and physiological response to demands that exceed perceived coping resources. The leading health risk in modern society. Small amounts of stress are healthy (eustress); chronic stress is harmful (distress).
Biopsychosocial Model
The framework understanding health as determined by Biological factors (genes, body), Psychological factors (thoughts, beliefs, stress), and Social factors (relationships, culture, environment).
Placebo Effect
A measurable health improvement produced by belief alone — without active treatment. Demonstrates that belief and mindset are real forces in physical health. People who didn't believe in their medication did not benefit from it.

Three Factors of Health

Biological Factors

Genes, body type, personality traits (linked to temperament), and inherited genetic conditions. Our family medical history is a map of potential health risks and strengths.

Social Factors

Social system, family relationships, community, and cultural beliefs. Beliefs are enormously powerful — if we believe we will fall ill, that belief can make us ill. If we believe we can be healthy, that belief supports actual health.

Psychological Factors

Lifestyle, stress levels, coping behaviors, personality, and temperament. Small stress is healthy — it builds new coping behaviors. Chronic, unmanaged stress damages health over time.

Risk Factors for Health

  1. Stress — the most significant modern health risk. Constant information overload (especially negative news and social media) produces negative emotions that settle in the body and can cause illness.
  2. Weight Management — our relationship with body and appearance is primarily mental. Distorted thinking about weight drives unhealthy behaviors.
  3. Smoking — harmful to body and mind. A habit built on the belief that it provides relief — a belief that can be replaced with healthier alternatives.
  4. Behavioral Choices — who we spend time with and the environments we place ourselves in have delayed but real health consequences.

Psychosomatic Conditions

Psychosomatic symptoms are real physical symptoms with psychological origins. They are not "imagined" — the body genuinely produces them. The source is in the emotional and mental state.

Symptom Possible Psychological Origin
Chronic pain (no physical cause) Unexpressed anger, grief, or unresolved trauma
High blood pressure Chronic stress, suppressed emotion, anxiety
Skin disorders (eczema, psoriasis) Emotional stress, anxiety, relationship conflict
Heart palpitations Anxiety, fear, emotional overwhelm
Headaches / migraines Mental tension, perfectionism, suppressed emotion
Digestive problems Anxiety ("gut feelings"), stress activation of gut

What to Do for Health

  • Physical exercise: Movement helps understand the body, gives the mind new information about itself, and supports both physical and mental health.
  • Healthy communication: If a communication pattern causes negative feelings, change it. New communication creates new feelings and new self-understanding.
  • Emotional intelligence: Understand and work with your emotions — this directly supports both mind and body.
  • Know your family medical history: Genetic inheritance provides important information about health risks.
  • Adequate rest: Give your body rest before it forces you to rest through illness.
  • Social connection: Healthy relationships are a proven protective factor against illness.

⭐ Must Memorize

  • Key insight: All illness begins in the mind — negative unexpressed emotions → physical symptoms
  • Psychosomatics: psyche (mind) + soma (body) = real physical symptoms from psychological causes
  • Biopsychosocial model: Biological + Psychological + Social = health outcomes
  • Placebo effect: belief alone can produce measurable physical healing
  • Stress: leading modern health risk; small stress = healthy (eustress); chronic = harmful (distress)
  • 4 risk factors: Stress, Weight, Smoking, Behavior
  • 5 goals of health psychology: understand, prevent, improve communication, apply medical knowledge, manage genetic risk

    Model Answer

    Psychosomatics refers to real physical symptoms that originate from psychological causes — stress, negative emotions, or unresolved psychological conflict. The term combines psyche (mind) and soma (body), reflecting the understanding that mind and body are inseparable. Two examples: (1) Chronic back pain with no physical cause — often linked to suppressed anger or grief; (2) High blood pressure — associated with chronic stress and emotional suppression. Social factors include family relationships, community support, cultural norms, and socioeconomic conditions. Health is not determined by biology alone — the mind and social environment are equally important.

Values, Norms, and Social Influence

The invisible rules that shape who we are and how we live together

Lecture Overview

All of our life is shaped by values, norms, and beliefs. We live in a social environment that functions through shared agreements about what matters and how to behave. This lecture explores what values and norms are, where they come from, how they change, and how they shape our social identity and conflicts.

Using Transactional Analysis (TA) and the Karpman Drama Triangle, this lecture provides tools for understanding social dynamics — and for identifying which role we play in interactions. This has direct relevance to everyday student life, family relationships, and online behavior.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Values
Deep-seated beliefs about what is important and good. They are behavioral guidelines — not goals, but the principles that shape communication, friendships, thinking, and social identity. Two meanings: (1) an object/activity representing a position, (2) a belief — the basis on which we act.
Norms
Evaluative beliefs about appropriate behavior — what is right and wrong in a given social context. Provide structure for communities. Without norms, every community collapses into continuous misunderstanding.
Social Environment
The social context in which communication between people occurs — shaping beliefs, values, norms, roles, and identities. Understanding someone's social environment means understanding what they believe, value, and expect.
Transactional Analysis (TA)
A psychological theory of social behavior describing three ego states: Child, Parent, and Adult. Each state represents a different way of experiencing and responding to the world.
Karpman Drama Triangle
A model of three dysfunctional roles in conflict communication: Victim, Persecutor, and Rescuer. The triangle is self-sustaining — each role reinforces the others.
Spiritual Basis of Personality
The set of goals, meanings, values, interests, norms, and beliefs that give our life direction and purpose. Includes relationships with others, nature, time, and ourselves.

Transactional Analysis: Three Social Positions

Position Behavior Pattern Relationship to Responsibility Conflict Risk
Child Always wants; manipulates others into meeting needs; avoids doing anything for themselves Avoids responsibility Very high
Parent Believes they know everything; sets rules for others; needs control Takes too much responsibility for others High
Adult Knows own needs; understands what must be done; takes responsibility for their own life Balanced personal responsibility Very low — healthiest position

We shift between positions depending on situation. The Adult position is the most psychologically healthy and conflict-free.

The Karpman Drama Triangle

Victim

Does not take responsibility. Uses weakness to manipulate Rescuers and attract Persecutors. Feels helpless and believes others are responsible for their suffering. Chooses their Persecutor and Rescuer from the social environment.

Rescuer

Wants to help everyone at the expense of their own needs. Loses themselves in helping others — which harms their own health, family, and relationships. Drawn into conflict with both Victim and Persecutor.

Persecutor

Creates problems for the Victim without full awareness. Driven by values oriented toward change, but others cannot understand this — conflict results. Often unaware of their impact.

To exit the triangle: ask Why am I in this communication? What does it give me? If it is not beneficial, change it or communicate honestly about your position.

Values, Norms, and Social Change

Values shape norms. Norms shape behavior. When values change — through migration, education, or new social environments — norms and behavior change with them. This explains why Kazakhstani youth navigate between traditional family values and modern global norms simultaneously.

Key Aspects of Social Environment

  • Economy: Communication involving money and material resources
  • Employment and Community: The need to belong to a group
  • Freedom of Choice: Ability to choose our own rules and direction
  • Security: The basic need to protect our life
  • Learning Opportunities: The need to grow and acquire knowledge
  • Arts and Culture: Understanding who we are and where we come from

Real-Life & Kazakhstan Context

Kazakhstan Youth

Kazakhstani young people often navigate between collectivist values (family honor, group harmony, elder respect) and individualist global norms (personal ambition, self-expression, digital identity). This creates productive tension — and sometimes internal conflict (intrapersonal conflict, Lecture 9).

Digital Social Norms

Online communities create their own norms rapidly — what is acceptable to post, how to respond, what deserves attention. These can conflict with offline values, creating the "online self vs offline self" tension studied in cyberpsychology.

University Drama Triangle

The Karpman Triangle plays out in academic group projects: the Victim complains they can't do their part, the Rescuer does everyone's work, the Persecutor sets impossible standards. Recognizing these roles is the first step to exiting them.

  • Values = behavioral guidelines (not goals); beliefs that shape how we live
  • Norms = evaluative beliefs about appropriate behavior; social structure
  • 3 TA positions: Child (manipulates, avoids responsibility), Parent (controls), Adult (healthy, responsible)
  • Karpman Triangle: Victim + Rescuer + Persecutor — self-sustaining cycle
  • Values change when social environment changes
  • Spiritual basis: values + norms + beliefs + meanings = direction and purpose in life
  • Helping others is central to spiritual health (confirmed by research)

Communication and Interpersonal Relationships

The art and science of connecting with others

Lecture Overview

Communication is one of the most important topics in this course and one of the most universally applicable skills in life. We communicate every day — with ourselves, with others, in formal settings, and through digital media. This lecture covers the full architecture of communication: what it is, its types and categories, its barriers, and the principles and techniques that make communication effective.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Communication
The process of exchanging information between people using various methods. Requires at least two parties: a sender and a recipient. Communication is cumulative — first impressions persist in future interactions.
Verbal Communication
Communication using spoken or written language. Includes oral (face-to-face, telephone), and written (letters, email, internet content).
Non-Verbal Communication
Communication without words — through body language, gestures, facial expressions, eye contact, space, touch, humor, and visual symbols. Accounts for approximately 18-19% of understood meaning in interpersonal communication (and much more in emotional contexts).
Active Listening
The practice of fully concentrating on, understanding, and responding to what another person is saying — without rushing to speak. The most powerful communication skill.
Feedback
The recipient's response to a message, which completes the communication loop. Without feedback, communication is one-way transmission, not true exchange.

The Communication Process

  1. Sender encodes a message in words, gestures, or signals
  2. Message travels through a channel (visual, auditory, tactile)
  3. Recipient decodes the message in their mind
  4. Feedback is sent back to the sender
  5. Sender adjusts based on feedback

Communication is a two-way loop. First impressions are stored and shape all future interaction — communication is cumulative.

Types of Communication

Type Methods Examples Strengths
Verbal Spoken language, written language Conversation, email, books, SMS Precise, documented, accessible
Non-Verbal Body language, gestures, eye contact, space, touch Nod, posture, eye contact, handshake Conveys emotion, tone, relationship
Visual Images, charts, diagrams, symbols Infographics, presentations, signs Fast comprehension, universal

Barriers to Communication

  • Language: Different languages or vocabularies create obvious barriers
  • Context: Misunderstanding the situation or tone (e.g., mistaking sarcasm for sincerity)
  • Time: Insufficient time for clear communication
  • Illness: Physical or emotional illness impairs ability to communicate and understand
  • Beliefs: Conflicting beliefs create defensiveness and breakdown
  • Emotions: Emotional mismatch (one person positive, one negative) makes mutual understanding difficult

Four Personality Types in Communication

Driver / Leader

Highly motivated, action-oriented, always wants to be first and achieve results. Communicate directly; they want efficiency and outcomes.

Influencer

Energetic; focused on motivating others. Positive, enthusiastic, creative, and encouraging. They want to inspire, not just lead.

Analyst

Focused on details, careful thinking, and understanding before acting. They want data, not just opinions.

Supporter / Relator

Warm, friendly, loyal. Prioritizes harmony and close relationships. May struggle with conflict or change.

Seven Principles of Effective Communication (The 7 C's)

  1. Clear — always give correct, accurate information
  2. Consistent — be open and reliable
  3. Considerate — help others find their direction in communication
  4. Complete — provide full, important information
  5. Relevant — say things appropriate to the situation
  6. Informative — be as informative as the situation requires
  7. Trustworthy — speak honestly in plain language others understand

Top 10 Communication Skills

  1. Emotional Intelligence — understand your own emotions and those of others
  2. Clarity — communicate clearly and informatively
  3. Friendly Tone — calm, pleasant, as if speaking to a friend
  4. Confidence — communicate in a way others can trust
  5. Empathy — understand others' positions and show you care
  6. Respect — find something good to acknowledge before criticism
  7. Active Listening — truly hear others without rushing to speak
  8. Open-Mindedness — listen even to things you think you know
  9. Asking Questions — open questions draw out others' thoughts
  10. Appropriate Language — suited to the person and context

10 Basic Communication Techniques

  1. Establish eye contact at the start
  2. Use open body posture — open hands, relaxed stance
  3. Maintain appropriate physical distance (closer with trusted people)
  4. Use the other person's name — one of the most powerful words in any interaction
  5. Ask questions about their situation and experience
  6. Share your own position and invite them to share theirs
  7. If you don't understand, ask again and show your confusion openly
  8. Ask open questions (not yes/no)
  9. Be honest
  10. Use appropriate language for the person and situation

⭐ Must Memorize

  • Communication = exchange of information; requires sender + recipient + feedback
  • 3 types: Verbal (spoken/written), Non-Verbal (body/gesture), Visual (images/charts)
  • Non-verbal = ~18-19% of understood meaning in interpersonal communication
  • 7 Principles: Clear, Consistent, Considerate, Complete, Relevant, Informative, Trustworthy
  • 4 personality types: Driver, Influencer, Analyst, Supporter
  • 6 barriers: Language, Context, Time, Illness, Beliefs, Emotions
  • Active listening = most powerful communication skill

Group Communication and Effective Communication

From one-to-one to many — how groups think, decide, and grow

Lecture Overview

This lecture extends the study of communication from interpersonal (two people) to group settings (three or more). Group communication is more complex and rewarding — groups generate collective intelligence beyond any individual, provide belonging, and establish shared identity and purpose.

The lecture also distinguishes interpersonal communication at three levels (object-object, subject-object, subject-subject) and provides practical guidance on communicating effectively with groups — from university seminars to professional teams.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Interpersonal Communication
The process of exchanging messages between two people, shaped by social and cultural norms. True interpersonal communication is Subject-to-Subject: both parties are active senders and receivers.
Group Communication
Communication among three or more people who share a goal, context, or social identity. More challenging than interpersonal communication — must reach multiple people simultaneously, and each person interprets differently.
Group Dynamics
The psychological and social processes occurring within and between groups — including roles, norms, cohesion, leadership, and conflict. Pioneered by Kurt Lewin.
Collective Intelligence
The enhanced decision-making and problem-solving capacity that emerges when a group pools diverse perspectives. Groups produce ideas beyond any single member's capability.

Three Levels of Communication

Level Participants Feedback? Example
Object ↔ Object Two non-human entities None Computer sends signal to printer
Subject → Object Person + thing One-way Person searches the internet
Subject ↔ Subject Two active people Full two-way Face-to-face conversation; true interpersonal communication

Interpersonal Communication: Key Characteristics

  • Allows sharing of thoughts, ideas, and feelings in ways group communication does not
  • Requires a specific time and place
  • Shaped by context, culture, and relationship between the parties
  • Can be continuously improved through practice and knowledge
  • Non-verbal cues (eye contact, posture, clothing, gestures, voice tone, space) constitute approximately 18-19% of understood meaning

Group Communication

Group communication is more challenging because:

  • You must communicate with multiple people simultaneously
  • You cannot control each person's interpretation
  • You must prepare your message more carefully

Benefits of Group Communication

  • More ideas, thoughts, and perspectives than any individual can provide
  • Groups can change your mind and reveal new possibilities
  • Group membership fulfills the need to belong
  • Provides shared meaning and purpose
  • Develops shared identity and collective goals

The Group Communication Cycle

  1. Need for connection
  2. Shared Goal
  3. Group Development
  4. New Ideas emerge
  5. Self-Esteem rises
  6. Personal Growth

Group vs. Team: Key Comparison

Dimension Group Team
Definition People with a shared context or identity People with a shared goal and coordinated roles
Interdependence Lower Higher — members depend on each other
Accountability Individual Mutual
Synergy May or may not emerge Intentionally created — output > sum of parts
Purpose Shared identity, belonging Shared goal, shared outcome

Online Groups & Cyberpsychology

Online Group Identity

Online communities (Discord servers, Telegram groups, Reddit communities) form rapid shared identity and norms — often stronger than geographic communities. Members feel genuine belonging despite never meeting physically.

Computer-Mediated Communication

Without non-verbal cues, misinterpretation rises dramatically. Written text carries none of the tone, warmth, or context of face-to-face communication — explaining why online conflict escalates so rapidly.

Group Projects at University

Effective group communication in university projects requires: clear role assignment, regular feedback loops, active listening, and explicit conflict-resolution norms. The highest-performing groups choose each other carefully and communicate psychological safety.

  • Group communication = 3+ people with shared context or goal
  • Group benefits: more ideas, belonging, shared identity, collective intelligence
  • Group vs Team: teams have higher interdependence, mutual accountability, intentional synergy
  • Key advice: before speaking to a group — write your speech first
  • Choose groups carefully — the right group accelerates your growth

    Model Answer

Psychology of Group and the Concept, Structure of Socio-Psychological Conflict

Understanding conflict as communication — and turning it constructive

Lecture Overview

Conflict is unavoidable — it is a natural part of human communication. This lecture reframes conflict: not as something bad to be avoided, but as a form of communication that can open minds, generate new decisions, and produce growth — if handled constructively. We explore the types, causes, triggers, and structure of conflict, and provide tools for transforming destructive conflict into constructive exchange.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Conflict
A situation — with specific time, place, and people — revealing a difference between individuals in needs, interests, goals, or values. A form of communication. Always has a goal and always produces a result (constructive or destructive).
Constructive Conflict
Conflict in which parties want to understand each other, avoid negative language, and seek shared goals. Produces new understanding and opens new paths. Healthy and necessary.
Destructive Conflict
Conflict for its own sake — the goal is to express anger or frustration, show position, use negative language. The only outcome is damage to the relationship and to oneself.
Conflict Genes
Specific words, actions, or behaviors that initiate conflict — often without the initiator being fully aware. Three types: Words, Actions, and Deeds.
Intrapersonal Conflict
Conflict within oneself — when you need or want one thing but must do another. Present in all people regularly. Example: wanting to sleep but needing to study.

Types of Conflict

Type Definition Example
Interpersonal Between two people; incompatible needs, goals, or positions Two classmates disagree on how to complete a project
Intrapersonal Within yourself; want one thing, must do another Wanting to sleep vs. needing to study for an exam
Unconscious Between conscious and unconscious mind; blocked needs surface indirectly Recurring dreams about an unresolved relationship
Intergroup Between groups with different values, interests, or resources Different departments competing for budget

Four Areas of Conflict

Economic Conflicts

Involving money, resources, or material interests. Who gets what, who pays, who benefits.

Ideological Conflicts

From incompatible beliefs, cultural values, religions, or political perspectives. These include conflicts between nationalities and worldviews.

Social Conflicts

Everyday misunderstandings in social environments — at university, in shops, on transport — arising from unmet expectations or misread signals.

Family Conflicts

Within family — about roles, boundaries, self-expression, and the transition from child to independent adult.

Conflict Triggers ("Conflict Genes")

  1. Words: Harsh, offensive, or hurtful language that activates conflict in others
  2. Actions: Promising something and not delivering — breaking trust and creating misunderstanding
  3. Deeds: Everyday behaviors and communication patterns that others cannot correctly interpret, leading to conflict

To reduce conflict: clearly explain what you mean by your words, actions, and behaviors.

Transactional Analysis in Conflict

Interaction Conflict Outcome
Child + Child Both want, neither gives — immediate and sustained conflict
Parent + Child Parent controls; Child resists — persistent conflict
Child + Adult Adult doesn't respond to manipulation; Child escalates — conflict results
Adult + Adult Only combination that allows communication without conflict

Constructive vs. Destructive Conflict

Dimension Constructive Conflict Destructive Conflict
Goal Mutual understanding, shared outcome Express position, vent emotion
Language Neutral, respectful Negative, attacking
Outcome New understanding, solution, growth Relationship damage, no resolution
Attitude Willingness to understand the other Only concerned with own position
Example "I want to understand what happened between us." "You always do this — it's always your fault."

To convert conflict to constructive: "I am very tired today because I didn't sleep well" — expressing your emotional state instead of attacking turns conflict into communication.

Conflict as a Dynamic Process

  1. Trigger / Conflict Gene activated
  2. Tension develops
  3. Open confrontation
  4. Resolution (constructive) or Escalation (destructive)
  5. Aftermath — new understanding or damaged relationship

Conflict is not static. It has a beginning, development, and result. Poor communication opens the door to conflict. Every conflict has a goal — and produces a result, constructive or destructive, depending on how it is handled.

⭐ Must Memorize

  • Conflict = form of communication; always has a goal + result
  • 3 types of conflict genes: Words, Actions, Deeds

🎓 Likely Exam Questions

  1. What is conflict in psychology? How does it differ from everyday understanding?

    Model Answer

    In everyday life, conflict is viewed negatively — as something to be avoided. In psychology, conflict is understood as a form of communication that reveals differences in needs, interests, goals, or values. It has a specific time, place, and participants, and always has a goal and a result. Conflict can be constructive — opening minds, generating new understanding, and producing solutions — or destructive, causing only damage. The key variable is how the conflict is handled: whether parties seek mutual understanding or only to express their own position.

  2. Distinguish between constructive and destructive conflict. How can conflict be made constructive?

    Model Answer

    Constructive conflict occurs when parties want to understand each other, use neutral/respectful language, and seek a shared outcome. It produces new understanding and solutions. Destructive conflict aims only to express anger or assert position, uses attacking language, and damages the relationship without resolution. To convert conflict to constructive: instead of attacking, express your emotional state ("I am very tired and overwhelmed right now") — this allows the other person to understand your position without feeling attacked, turning conflict into honest communication.

Meaning of Life & Psychological Well-being

Why we exist — and how to live fully

Lecture Overview

This final lecture brings the entire course together. Meaning of life, professional self-determination, and psychological well-being are interconnected: when we understand why we are here and what we are working toward, we have the fuel to live fully, grow continuously, and contribute to others.

Viktor Frankl's logotherapy, Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory, and Kazakhstan's Rukhani Zhangyru program all point in the same direction: meaning is found through family, work, and helping others — and it gives us the motivation to face every challenge life brings.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Meaning of Life
Your personal understanding of existence — your philosophy, spiritual position, or scientific self-understanding. Includes knowing what is good for you, what happiness means to you, and what you want your life to contribute. Changes as you grow.
Logotherapy
(Frankl) A form of psychotherapy based on the belief that the primary motivational force in humans is the search for meaning — not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler).
Self-Determination
Understanding your needs and motivation deeply enough to direct your own growth. Requires the three basic needs: Competence, Relatedness, and Autonomy (Deci & Ryan).
Psychological Well-being
(Ryff) A multi-dimensional concept including self-acceptance, positive relationships, environmental mastery, autonomy, purpose in life, and personal growth. Not merely the absence of illness.
Rukhani Zhangyru
Kazakhstan's national cultural and spiritual revival program. Presents stories of remarkable people as inspiration for personal development and self-determination. Encourages connection to cultural heritage and deeper self-understanding.
Eudaimonia
Aristotle's concept of living well and doing well — well-being as the result of living a meaningful, virtuous, and purposeful life. Distinguished from hedonia (pleasure-based well-being).

Viktor Frankl's Theory of Meaning

Frankl survived Nazi concentration camps and from that experience built logotherapy — the idea that the primary human motivation is not pleasure or power, but the search for meaning. Without meaning, we cannot live fully or understand our problems correctly.

Three Sources of Meaning (Frankl)

1. Family

Living for and through our family relationships. Many people find their deepest purpose in love and family bonds. The desire to provide for and protect family gives life direction.

2. Work

Through work, we earn resources, discover interests, develop skills, and contribute to the world. Work done with purpose becomes a profound source of meaning.

3. Helping Others

Frankl considered this the most enduring source. We can lose family; we can lose work. But the desire to help others never fully disappears. When we help others, we understand why we are here.

"The sole meaning of life is to serve humanity." — Viktor Frankl

Questions to Find Your Meaning of Life

  • Why is meaning of life important to me?
  • Who am I, and why am I here?
  • What is the original purpose of life for me?
  • What interests me deeply, and why?
  • What is the value of my life?
  • What is the real life I want to live?
  • What is my reason to live?
  • What do I live for each day?

Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

Professional self-determination requires understanding your needs and motivation. The three basic needs:

Competence

The need to be effective — to learn, master skills, and understand what we need. When competence is met, we feel capable and motivated.

Relatedness

The need for close relationships — feeling part of a group, knowing others need us as we need them. Loneliness undermines meaning and well-being.

Autonomy

The need to feel that we are growing and developing freely — that our life is fully lived according to our values, not external pressure.

Two Keys of Self-Determination

  1. The Need to Grow: All people need development. We cannot live without growth. Constantly asking questions, learning, and pursuing what interests us is itself a source of motivation.
  2. Meaning as Motivation: When we understand our meaning, it becomes our motivation. When we grow toward meaning, we understand our needs more deeply. This is a virtuous cycle.

Rukhani Zhangyru (Spiritual Revival)

Rukhani Zhangyru is Kazakhstan's national program for spiritual, cultural, and personal revival. In the context of self-determination and meaning:

  • Presents the stories of remarkable Kazakh leaders, thinkers, and contributors who achieved high self-determination
  • Inspires the belief: "I can do more for myself and for my life"
  • Encourages connection to cultural history and deeper self-understanding
  • Supports goal achievement, communication development, and a richer understanding of life's meaning
  • Connects individual meaning to collective cultural identity — vital for Kazakhstani youth

Psychological Well-being (Ryff's Model)

Self-Acceptance

Positive regard for oneself and one's past. Accepting both strengths and limitations without harsh self-judgment.

Positive Relations

Warm, trusting, close relationships with others. Genuine care and connection.

Autonomy

Self-direction, independence, resisting social pressure. Living according to personal standards.

Environmental Mastery

Competence in managing daily life and complex environments. Using opportunities effectively.

Purpose in Life

Having goals, direction, and a sense of meaning. Feeling life has significance.

Personal Growth

Openness to new experiences. Continuously developing and realizing one's potential.

🎓 Likely Exam Questions

  1. What is Viktor Frankl's theory of meaning? What are his three sources of meaning?

    Model Answer

    Viktor Frankl, founder of logotherapy, argued that the primary motivational force in humans is the search for meaning — not pleasure (as Freud argued) or power (Adler). Without meaning, we cannot live fully, understand our problems correctly, or realize our potential. Frankl identified three sources of meaning: (1) Family — living for and through our deepest relationships; (2) Work — discovering purpose through contribution and skill development; (3) Helping Others — the most enduring source; we may lose family or work, but the drive to help others never disappears. Living in the present moment — fully aware of what we feel and want right now — is also central to Frankl's approach.

  2. What is Self-Determination Theory? Name the three basic needs and explain why they matter for well-being.

    Model Answer

    Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan) holds that intrinsic motivation and psychological well-being depend on meeting three basic psychological needs. Competence — the need to be effective and master skills — provides a sense of capability and motivation to grow. Relatedness — the need for close relationships and belonging — ensures we feel connected and valued. Autonomy — the need to self-direct our growth according to our own values — gives us freedom and authentic engagement. When all three needs are met, people are intrinsically motivated, psychologically healthy, and able to pursue meaningful goals. When any need is chronically unmet, well-being and motivation suffer.

  3. What is Rukhani Zhangyru and how does it relate to meaning of life and self-determination?

    Model Answer

    Rukhani Zhangyru is Kazakhstan's national program for spiritual and cultural revival. In the psychological context of meaning and self-determination, it serves several functions: it presents the stories of remarkable Kazakhstani individuals who achieved high self-determination, inspiring the belief that "I can do more for my life." It encourages people to connect with their cultural history and national identity, which contributes to a sense of meaning rooted in something larger than the individual. The program supports goal achievement, communication development, and a richer understanding of life's meaning — connecting personal psychological development to collective cultural flourishing.
